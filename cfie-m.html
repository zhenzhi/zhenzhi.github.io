Summary

CFIE is a Chinese food recipe mobile app. This app can teach people interested in cooking Chinese food how to cook easily and fast. It also have many features related to food like helath news and shopping list. In a world, CFIE - The end of kitchen disaster.

Problems and Goals

The number of Chinese students enrolling in American universities has grown to nearly a quarter million and preparing meals that remind them of home can be difficult and time-consuming for a number of reasons, such as limited availability of Chinese ingredients, the complexity of recipes, limited transportation options, and searching inefficiencies on existing recipe apps. The Chinese food is cooked with the intention of increasing the health benefits like longevity and having healing powers and medicinal value. More and more people like Chinese food and they think Chinese food is hard to make. But actually Chinese food could be easy. We want to make an application easier to use for people who interested in cooking Chinese food.

Process

Contextual Inquiry and Interviews

2 people were observed when they were cooking by the direction of recipe. In the contextual inquiry part, we are more focus on how people using recipe when they are cooking. 4 people including Chinese and American students were interviewed. We have gathered data about how do they cook and what kind of problems they met during the cooking.

Competitive Analysis

We have used competitive analysis method to compare 5 different recipe apps including two Chinese version and two American version. In this part, we are more focused on the navugation, search process and engaging elements of the app.

Usability testing

We conducted usability testing to inform navigation and content design decisions. Specifically, we were interested in how test participants navigated the homepage and search results pages, their expectations regarding filter options, and how users expected to engage with the site. Our test participants ranged in age from 22-38, four were Chinese graduate students, four were graduate students from the United States, and five of them typically cook for themselves. Participants were asked background questions to ascertain their general cooking habits, how they search for recipes, and interest in using recipe websites. They were then asked to provide their opinion on the homepage format and visible features. Users were then tasked with finding a new recipe. We observed whether they chose to search by keyword or to browse and then to provide feedback on which options on the results page would help them choose a recipe from the results.
